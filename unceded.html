Unceded (Ongoing)

This work explores land grabbing as a tool for systematic marginalization of indigenous people in three hill districts of Bangladesh – Bandarban, Rangamati, and Khagrachhari – collectively called Chittagong Hill Tracts (CHT).

To deprive a group of people of their rights, the first step is often to make them powerless, usually by seizing their resources. And for indigenous people, land is the most vital resource, as their lives are inseparable from nature. The first significant displacement in CHT occurred in 1962 with the construction of the Kaptai Dam submerging 40% of the area's cultivable land and displacing 100,000 people. Since then, indigenous land has been taken away by the state, the public, and also by law enforcement groups.

For this project, I chose the aesthetic of Kodak Aerochrome, a technology developed during WWII by a collaboration between Kodak and the US military to detect camouflaged enemy troops. By reversing its gaze, the technology is used not to hunt, but to reveal something that is intentionally being overlooked.
